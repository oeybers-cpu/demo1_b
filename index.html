Introduction

James Paul Gee (2010): A Situated Sociocultural Approach to Literacy and Technology

James Paul Gee's (2010) seminal work offers a transformative framework for understanding literacy in the digital age. Gee synthesizes insights from New Literacy Studies, Situated Cognition Studies, and New Media Literacy Studies to argue that literacy is not merely a cognitive skill but a socially and culturally situated practice. According to Gee, literacy must be understood as participation in "Discourses" (with a capital D), which are socially accepted ways of using language, thinking, believing, and acting in particular contexts. This perspective moves beyond viewing literacy as simple decoding, positioning it as social identity and cultural membership.

Gee demonstrates how different social groups develop distinct "literacies" tied to their practices. Reading a Yu-Gi-Oh card requires not just linguistic competence but understanding gaming community norms. This pluralistic view of "multiple literacies" challenges traditional educational models that privilege academic language while marginalizing popular culture literacy practices. A key contribution is "situated meaning," where understanding emerges from active engagement in meaningful activities. Video game players develop comprehension not by reading manuals alone, but by playing and experiencing how language connects to action, creating "lucidly functional language" tied to concrete experiences.
